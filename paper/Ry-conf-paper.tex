\section{Introduction}

Conformal invariance as a symmetry of critical fluctuations was first conjectured by Polyakov in 1970 \cite{Polyakov:1970xd}. This idea ended up quite influential and led to the development of the conformal field theory (CFT), by Polyakov and others \cite{Polyakov:1974gs,Belavin:1984vu}. By now it is widely believed that a great majority of critical points---both thermodynamic and quantum---possess an emergent conformal invariance and their long-distance behavior is described by a CFT \cite{Cardy1987,Cardy:1996xt,tsvelik,giamarchi2003quantum,sachdev,fradkin}.\footnote{Some well understood reasons \cite{Polchinski:1987dy,Nakayama:2013is} do allow rare exceptions---critical points with only scale symmetry.} CFTs are tightly constrained and can be solved, in low dimensions often exactly \cite{DiFrancesco:1997nk}, while in higher dimensions approximately using the numerical conformal bootstrap \cite{Poland:2018epd}. Scaling dimensions of CFT local operators are related to the critical exponents, which can be experimentally measured, providing many indirect tests of conformality,\footnote{We will use `conformal symmetry', `conformal invariance' and `conformality' interchangeably.} reviewed e.g.~in \cite{Henkel1999,Rychkov:2025zks}. In comparison, until recently little theoretical effort was devoted to designing direct tests \cite{Pokrovskii1973,Gompper1985}, and such experiments were lacking \cite{Rychkov:2025zks}. In thermodynamic phase transitions such tests are limited by imaging possibilities with X-ray or neutron probes, although see \cite{Podo:2026hfh} for a recent proposal to use grazing scattering of X-rays on critical binary alloys.

In the meantime, cold atom platforms revolutionized the experimental study of quantum phase transitions \cite{}. They allow to engineer many quantum Hamiltonians of interest and study their quantum critical points \cite{}. These experiments can measure the state of every atom, thus providing a snapshot view of the many-body quantum state. Averaging over many snapshots allows to measure equal-time correlation function \cite{}---a classic way to detect conformal invariance \cite{Polyakov:1970xd}.

The time is ripe to give a general discussion of existing and future tests of conformality in cold atom experiments, which is our purpose here. Our paper has two main parts. In Section \ref{sec:gen}, we propose our view of which tests should count as indirect and which ones are direct. Indirect tests are measurements of critical exponents, while direct tests need to verify that correlation functions satisfy a conformal Ward identity. We illustrate these points by examples of existing experiments. 

Then, in Section \ref{sec:prop},  we discuss the simplest setups to test conformality through measurements of equal-time two-point correlation functions, with periodic and open boundary conditions. We present results of our numerical simulations for experiments with 1D chains of Rydberg atoms. The tests we imagine have not yet been performed due to limitations of decoherence and/or insufficient adiabaticity of ground state preparation, but we hope they are within reach. In Section \ref{sec:concl} we conclude.

\section{Tests of conformal invariance}
\label{sec:gen}

\subsection{General discussion}
Experimental tests of conformality fall into two groups - indirect and direct.

Suppose we want to check rotational symmetry of a 3D object - is it a round ball? The direct way would be to stare at it from different directions. An indirect way would be to check if the circumference to diameter ratio equals $\pi$. 
The point of this analogy is that symmetry is fundamentally about shapes of objects. In a quantum field theory the most basic object is a correlation function. Hence, a direct test of conformality should test constraints which it imposes on the functional form of correlators. On the other hand, an indirect test can be a measurement of some number predicted using techniques of CFT. In fact specific CFTs can often be solved exactly, or numerically to very high accuracy. For example, an infinite series of 2d CFTs called minimal models was solved exactly, determining the local operator scaling dimensions and other properties \cite{Belavin:1984vu,DiFrancesco:1997nk}. The main critical exponents characterizing phase transitions can be expressed in terms of these scaling dimensions \cite{cardy1996scaling}. Hence, any measurement of a critical experiment in agreement with a CFT prediction is an indirect test of conformality. Many such indirect tests can be extracted from the literature \cite{Henkel1999,Rychkov:2025zks}, even though the original experimental papers may not have emphasized the connection with conformality.

Talking about CFT correlators, we should specify the geometry, including the signature: Euclidean or Lorentzian. As a representative example, consider the 2d Ising CFT, i.e. the $M(3,4)$ minimal model, which has local primary operators $=\sigma,\epsilon$ of scaling dimensions $\Delta_\sigma=1/8$, $\Delta_\epsilon=1$. Then, in infinite flat 2d space, the simplest nontrivial correlators are two-point (2pt) and thee-point (3pt) functions of primary operators:
\begin{align}
	\langle \mathcal{O}(x_1) \mathcal{O}(x_2)\rangle_{\mathbb{R}^2} &= \frac{1}{x_{12}^{2\Delta_\mathcal{O}}}\qquad (\mathcal{O}=\sigma,\epsilon)\,,
	\label{eq:two-point}\\
	\langle \sigma(x_1) \sigma(x_2)\epsilon(x_3)\rangle_{\mathbb{R}^2} &= \frac{C_{\sigma\sigma\epsilon}}{x_{12}^{2\Delta_\sigma-\Delta_\epsilon}
x_{31}^{\Delta_\epsilon} x_{32}^{\Delta_\epsilon}}\,,\quad C_{\sigma\sigma\epsilon}=1/2. \label{eq:three-point}
	\end{align}
	Here $x_{ij}$ are either the Euclidean or the Lorentzian distances between $x_i$ and $x_j$, depending on the signature of the space.\footnote{In the latter case, the distances are computed with infinitesimal imaginary parts for time differences, depending on the operator ordering, giving phases for light-like point separations \cite{Hofman:2008ar}.}
	
	\begin{figure}[ht]
		\centering
	   \includegraphics[width=200pt]{fig-flat.pdf}
		\caption{Direct tests in infinite space: 2pt functions (left) test but scale symmetry, while 3pt functions (right) do test comformality but are hard to measure.}%
		\label{fig:flat}
	\end{figure}
	
	The direct/indirect separation works as follows. To measure $\Delta_\sigma$, $\Delta_\epsilon$, or $C_{\sigma\sigma\epsilon}$ in agreement with their exact CFT values would be an indirect test of conformality. To measure the 2pt functions \eqref{eq:two-point} and see a powerlaw would be a direct test albeit not of the conformal but of the more mundane scale symmetry.\footnote{2pt functions of primaries of unequal dimensions vanish by conformality \cite{Polyakov:1970xd}, but it's unclear how to test this experimentally. See \cite{Podo:2026hfh} for a critique of an early proposal by Pokrovsky \cite{Pokrovskii1973,PatashinskiPokrovsky1979}.}  Finally, to measure the 3pt function \eqref{eq:three-point} would be a direct test of conformality. Indeed, this specific functional form in terms of just three parameters $\Delta_\sigma,\Delta_\epsilon,C_{\sigma\sigma\epsilon}$ was one of the earliest nontrivial predictions of conformal symmetry \cite{Polyakov:1970xd}, obtained from covariance under conformal maps.
	Scale symmetry alone would allow here an infinite number of possibilities \cite{Rychkov:2016iqz}.
	Measurements of infinite-volume 3pt functions are complicated, and it was never attempted.\footnote{We thank Thomas Chalopin and David Cl\'ement for a discussion of the possibility of realizing such a measurement in their experimental cold-atom platform \cite{Allemand:2025pdq}.} See Fig.~\ref{fig:flat}.
	
	Let us move to finite volume case, which is most relevant for the Rydberg atom experiments, typically done on groups of 20-200 atoms grouped in a 1D or 2D array. As we wish to compare with 2d CFT, we consider the 1D setup---a chain of Rydberg atoms arranged either as a circle or as an interval, whose interactions are adjusted to be at the quantum critical point. This corresponds to CFT on, respectively, the ``cylinder'' or the ``strip'' geometry (see Fig.~\ref{fig:cyl}), with time running vertically.  
	
		\begin{figure}[ht]
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=200pt]{fig-cyl.pdf}
		\caption{Direct tests of conformality in finite volume, discussed in the text. Left: 2pt function on the cylinder. Right: 1pt function on the strip with identical boundary conditions.}%
		\label{fig:cyl}
	\end{figure}
	
	While scale symmetry is broken by finite volume, conformality survives. In fact conformal group for the 2d cylinder is as large as that of the plane, while for the strip it's the same as for the half-plane. This results in constraints on correlators.  As the simplest nontrivial example, consider the 2pt function of a primary on the cylinder \cite{Luscher:1974ez}:
	\beq
	\label{eq:2ptcyl}
	\langle \mathcal{O}(\phi_1,\tau_1) \mathcal{O}(\phi_2,\tau_2)\rangle_{\text{cyl}}  = 
	\frac{1}{|2 \cosh(\tau_1-\tau_2) - 2 \cos(\phi_1-\phi_2)|^{\Delta_\cO}}\,,
		\eeq
		where $\phi_i\in [0,2\pi]$, $\tau_i\in \mathbb{R}$. This is for the Euclidean cylinder. The 2pt function is time-translation invariant, and decays exponentially at large time separation. This expression can be easily found mapping the cylinder to the plane by an exponential map, and using the infinite-plane result \eqref{eq:two-point} \cite{DiFrancesco:1997nk}. But, for the sake of the argument, one can also derive it independently by staying on the cylinder and using its conformal group. 
		
		
		For the Hamiltonian dynamics in real time, like in Rydberg atom experiments, one needs to analytically continue \eqref{eq:2ptcyl} to Lorentzian signature. Then the 2pt function exhibits oscillating behavior in the time direction \cite{Luscher:1974ez}. Below we will focus on the equal time case, in which case the 2pt function reduces to
	\beq
	\label{eq:2ptcylequal}
	\langle \mathcal{O}(\phi_1,0) \mathcal{O}(\phi_2,0)\rangle_{\text{cyl}}  = \frac 1{|2\sin\frac{\phi_1-\phi_2}2|^{\Delta_\cO}},
	\eeq
for both the Euclidean and Lorentzian signature.
	
	 In the same spirit, we can consider correlation functions on a strip, assuming conformal boundary conditions $B_1$ and $B_2$ on the two boundaries. In this case, already 1pt functions can test conformal symmetry. In the simplest case with equal boundary conditions, $B_1=B_2$, the 1pt function of a primary operator on the strip of width $\pi$ takes the form:
	 \beq
	 \label{eq:1ptstrip}
	 \langle \mathcal{O}(\phi,0)\rangle_{\text{strip}}  = \frac {A_{\mathcal{O}}}
	 {(2 \sin \phi)^{\Delta_\cO}}\,, \quad 0<\phi<\pi\,\quad(B_1=B_2)\,.
	 \eeq
	 This is usually shown conformal-transforming the upper-half-plane 1pt function $\langle \mathcal{O}(z)\rangle_{\text{UHP}} =  {A_{\mathcal{O}}}/(2\, \text{Im} z)^{\Delta_\cO}$ \cite{Cardy:1984bb} to the strip \cite{Calabrese:2006rx}. But it can also be derived by staying on the strip.
	  
	 For a nontrivial test, $A_\cO$ in \eqref{eq:1ptstrip} should not vanish due to global symmetry reasons. For example, for $\cO=\sigma$ in the 2d Ising CFT, the boundary condition should break global $\mathbb{Z}_2$, hence it should be either ``spin-up'' or ``spin-down'' boundary condition $|+\rangle$, $|-\rangle$ and not the ``free'' boundary condition $|f\rangle$.
	 
	 In summary, it is because of conformality that the functional form of \eqref{eq:2ptcyl}, \eqref{eq:1ptstrip} is fixed for all angles and not just in the short-distance limit where operators approach each other or the boundary, where it follows by scale symmetry. To see this functional form experimentally would be a direct test of conformality. In Section \ref{sec:prop} we will discuss further these two simplest direct tests. More complicated tests could be constructed using higher-point functions on the cylinder, or correlators with unequal boundary conditions on two sides of the strip or with boundary operators insertions. 
	
Let us next discuss another possible test of conformality, namely testing the operator-state correspondence (OSC). Consider all local operators of a CFT ${\cO}_i$, both primaries and their descendants. The OSC on the cylinder says that such operators are in one-to-one correspondence with the energy eigenstates on the circle, and that scaling dimensions and the excitation energies about the ground state are proportional to each other. There is also OSC for the strip with boundary conditions $B_1$ and $B_2$, between states on the interval and  boundary (or boundary-changing if $B_1\ne B_2$) operators $\widehat{\cO}_i$. 

The proportionality factor involves the ``speed of light'' of the emergent Lorentz invariance, as well as the physical length of space. To eliminate these factors, one can study the excitation energy ratios $E_i/E_j$. Then the prediction is 
\beq
\label{eq:ratio-rel}
E_i/E_j = \Delta_{\cO_i}/\Delta_{\cO_j}\,\quad\text{(cylinder)},
\eeq	
and a similar relation with $\Delta_{\cO_i}\to\Delta_{\widehat{\cO}_i}$ for the strip.

OSC is a fundamental property of CFT, which results because the cylinder and strip geometries are conformally equivalent to the plane and half-plane (see Fig.~\ref{fig:OSC}). Therefore, a test of relation \eqref{eq:ratio-rel} also qualifies as a direct test of conformality.\footnote{We thank the authors of \cite{Wang:2026prw} for a discussion.}

	\begin{figure}[ht]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=200pt]{fig-OSC.pdf}
	\caption{Justification of OSC. Cylinder, resp. strip, can be conformally mapped onto the plane (left), resp. half-plane (right), with constant time sections mapped to constant radius (semi)circles.}%
	\label{fig:OSC}
\end{figure}

 We note that the discussed finite volume tests of conformal invariance have no easy counterpart for 2D, where finite volume typically breaks conformality. The only exception is a sphere, but in cold atom experiments spherical symmetry will be broken to a discrete subgroup, which makes comparison to CFT subtle although not impossible \cite{Lao:2023zis,Wu:2026ayb}. Experimental realization of the recently proposed fuzzy sphere quantum criticality \cite{Zhu:2022gjc}, with electrons moving on the sphere with a magnetic flux, is currently lacking.

\subsection{Existing experimental results with Rydberg atoms}

Measurements of critical 2pt functions were performed by Refs.~\cite{Fang:2024uyf,Emperauger:2025raf}.
Ref.~\cite{Fang:2024uyf} studied the 2d Ising universality class with $N=24$ Rydberg atoms in a circle, while Ref.~\cite{Emperauger:2025raf} focused on the XY universality class in the same geometry.
Both works saw a short-distance powerlaw, followed by exponential decay with correlation length $\xi=13.2^{+5.7}_{-3.6}$ \cite{Fang:2024uyf} or $\xi=15(4)$ \cite{Emperauger:2025raf}, attributed to decoherence effects and other imperfections. Thus the direct test of conformality via Eq.~\eqref{eq:2ptcylequal} remains open. (See Section \ref{sec:prop} for possible improvements.) Still, from short-distance powerlaw they could extract scaling dimensions and compare with the theory. Ref.~\cite{Fang:2024uyf} saw $\Delta_\sigma=0.127(37)$ in agreement with the 2d Ising CFT, which counts as an indirect test of conformality. Ref.~\cite{Emperauger:2025raf} measured $\Delta\approx 0.13$, the vertex operator dimension in the compact scalar boson CFT at Luttinger parameter $K=1.6(4)$. Since $K$ is a modulus (unconstrained parameter), it's hard to interpret this result as an indirect test of conformality.

Measurements of excitation energies were performed in Ref.~\cite{Sun:2026aqf}, working on the interval geometry (a chain of $N=19$ Rydberg atoms), and using time-periodic modulation. They thus tested the OSC relation \eqref{eq:ratio-rel} for the strip. For the critical Ising model with spin-up boundary conditions on both sides, they measured low-lying excitation energies up to level $\sim7$, showing that they are are equally spaced, in agreement with the free Majorana theory description of the underlying CFT. They next studied the tricritical Ising (TCI) model with several boundary conditions. E.g.~for TCI with the free boundary conditions they measured $E_2/E_1=1.45(25)$ in agreement with the CFT prediction $4/3$. As the TCI boundary spectrum does not have a free theory description, this is a nontrivial direct test of conformality, perhaps the first one ever.

The measurements of Ref.~\cite{Fang:2024uyf,Emperauger:2025raf,Sun:2026aqf} were done with the critical ground state, prepared via with adiabatic evolution. Another setup is to drive a 1D \cite{Keesling:2018ish,Zhang:2025xkp} or 2D \cite{Ebadi:2020ldi,Manovitz:2024hif} array of Rydberg atoms through the critical point non-adiabatically, and extract critical exponents from the Kibble-Zurek scaling. As any exponent measurement, these count as indirect tests of conformality, including its prediction $z=1$ for the dynamical critical exponent. 

We also mention closely related digital quantum computer studies \cite{Anand:2022cdi,Dborin:2022zdd,Haghshenas:2023bje}. They first use a classical computer to find a holographic quantum circuit describing the critical state of interest, e.g.~the 2d Ising criticality. The circuit is then evaluated on a quantum computer, measuring e.g.~the 2pt function. While these works are not fully experimental tests of conformality in a quantum phase transition, they do demonstrate growing power of quantum computing.
