\section{Introduction}

Conformal invariance as a symmetry of critical fluctuations was first conjectured by Polyakov in 1970 \cite{Polyakov:1970xd}. This idea ended up quite influential and led to the development of the conformal field theory (CFT), by Polyakov and others \cite{Polyakov:1974gs,Belavin:1984vu}. By now it is widely believed that a great majority of critical points---both thermodynamic and quantum---possess an emergent conformal invariance and their long-distance behavior is described by a CFT \cite{Cardy1987,Cardy:1996xt,tsvelik,giamarchi2003quantum,sachdev,fradkin}.\footnote{Well-understood reasons \cite{Polchinski:1987dy,Nakayama:2013is} do allow rare exceptions---critical points with only scale symmetry.} CFTs are tightly constrained and can be solved, in low dimensions often exactly \cite{DiFrancesco:1997nk}, while in higher dimensions approximately using the numerical conformal bootstrap \cite{Poland:2018epd}. Scaling dimensions of CFT local operators are related to the critical exponents, which can be experimentally measured, providing many indirect tests of conformality,\footnote{We will use `conformal symmetry', `conformal invariance' and `conformality' interchangeably.} reviewed e.g.~in \cite{Henkel1999,Rychkov:2025zks}. In comparison, until recently little theoretical effort was devoted to designing direct tests \cite{Pokrovskii1973,Gompper1985}, and such experiments were lacking \cite{Rychkov:2025zks}. In thermodynamic phase transitions such tests are limited by imaging possibilities with X-ray or neutron probes, although see \cite{Podo:2026hfh} for a recent proposal to use grazing scattering of X-rays on critical binary alloys.

In the meantime, cold atom platforms \cite{Browaeys:2020kzz,Morgado:2020jfo} revolutionized the experimental study of quantum phase transitions \cite{Bernien:2017ubn,Scholl:2020hzx,Ebadi:2020ldi,Semeghini:2021wls}. They allow to engineer many quantum Hamiltonians of interest and study their quantum critical points \cite{Keesling:2018ish,Fang:2024uyf,Sun:2026aqf}. These experiments can measure the state of every atom, thus providing a snapshot view of the many-body quantum state. Averaging over many snapshots allows to measure equal-time correlation function \cite{Fang:2024uyf,Emperauger:2025raf}---a classic way to probe conformality \cite{Polyakov:1970xd}.

The time is ripe for a general discussion of existing and future tests of conformality in cold atom experiments---our purpose here. Our paper has two main parts. In Section \ref{sec:gen}, we propose our view of which tests should count as indirect and which ones are direct. Indirect tests are measurements of critical exponents, while direct tests need to verify that correlation functions satisfy a conformal Ward identity. We illustrate these points by examples of existing experiments. 

Then, in Section \ref{sec:prop},  we discuss the simplest setups to test conformality through measurements of equal-time two-point correlation functions, with periodic and open boundary conditions. We present results of our numerical simulations for experiments with 1D chains of Rydberg atoms. The tests we imagine have not yet been performed due to limitations of decoherence and/or insufficient adiabaticity of ground state preparation, but we hope they are within reach. In Section \ref{sec:concl} we conclude.

Notation: D for spatial dimension, d for full spacetime dimension, as is common in cond-mat and exp literature
\section{Tests of conformal invariance}
\label{sec:gen}

\subsection{General discussion}
Experimental tests of conformality fall into two groups - indirect and direct.

Suppose we want to check rotational symmetry of a 3D object - is it a round ball? The direct way would be to stare at it from different directions. An indirect way would be to check if the circumference to diameter ratio equals $\pi$. 
The point of this analogy is that symmetry is fundamentally about shapes of objects. In a quantum field theory the most basic object is a correlation function. Hence, a direct test of conformality should test constraints which it imposes on the functional form of correlators. On the other hand, an indirect test can be a measurement of some number predicted using techniques of CFT. In fact specific CFTs can often be solved exactly, or numerically to very high accuracy. For example, an infinite series of 2d CFTs called minimal models was solved exactly, determining the local operator scaling dimensions and other properties \cite{Belavin:1984vu,DiFrancesco:1997nk}. The main critical exponents characterizing phase transitions can be expressed in terms of these scaling dimensions \cite{cardy1996scaling}. Hence, any measurement of a critical experiment in agreement with a CFT prediction is an indirect test of conformality. Many such indirect tests can be extracted from the literature \cite{Henkel1999,Rychkov:2025zks}, even though the original experimental papers may not have emphasized the connection with conformality.

Talking about CFT correlators, we should specify the geometry, including the signature: Euclidean or Lorentzian. As a representative example, consider the 2d Ising CFT, i.e. the $\mathcal{M}(3,4)$ minimal model, which has local primary operators $=\sigma,\epsilon$ of scaling dimensions $\Delta_\sigma=1/8$, $\Delta_\epsilon=1$. Then, in infinite flat 2d space, the simplest nontrivial correlators are two-point (2pt) and thee-point (3pt) functions of primary operators:
\begin{align}
	\langle \mathcal{O}(x_1) \mathcal{O}(x_2)\rangle_{\mathbb{R}^2} &= \frac{1}{x_{12}^{2\Delta_\mathcal{O}}}\qquad (\mathcal{O}=\sigma,\epsilon)\,,
	\label{eq:two-point}\\
	\langle \sigma(x_1) \sigma(x_2)\epsilon(x_3)\rangle_{\mathbb{R}^2} &= \frac{C_{\sigma\sigma\epsilon}}{x_{12}^{2\Delta_\sigma-\Delta_\epsilon}
x_{31}^{\Delta_\epsilon} x_{32}^{\Delta_\epsilon}}\,,\quad C_{\sigma\sigma\epsilon}=1/2. \label{eq:three-point}
	\end{align}
	Here $x_{ij}$ are either the Euclidean or the Lorentzian distances between $x_i$ and $x_j$, depending on the signature of the space.\footnote{In the latter case, the distances are computed with infinitesimal imaginary parts for time differences, depending on the operator ordering, giving phases for light-like point separations \cite{Hofman:2008ar}.}
	
	\begin{figure}[ht]
		\centering
	   \includegraphics[width=200pt]{fig-flat.pdf}
		\caption{Direct tests in infinite space: 2pt functions (left) test but scale symmetry, while 3pt functions (right) do test comformality but are hard to measure.}%
		\label{fig:flat}
	\end{figure}
	
	The direct/indirect separation works as follows. To measure $\Delta_\sigma$, $\Delta_\epsilon$, or $C_{\sigma\sigma\epsilon}$ in agreement with their exact CFT values would be an indirect test of conformality. To measure the 2pt functions \eqref{eq:two-point} and see a powerlaw would be a direct test albeit not of the conformal but of the more mundane scale symmetry.\footnote{2pt functions of primaries of unequal dimensions vanish by conformality \cite{Polyakov:1970xd}, but it's unclear how to test this experimentally. See \cite{Podo:2026hfh} for a critique of an early proposal by Pokrovsky \cite{Pokrovskii1973,PatashinskiPokrovsky1979}.}  Finally, to measure the 3pt function \eqref{eq:three-point} would be a direct test of conformality. Indeed, this specific functional form in terms of just three parameters $\Delta_\sigma,\Delta_\epsilon,C_{\sigma\sigma\epsilon}$ was one of the earliest nontrivial predictions of conformal symmetry \cite{Polyakov:1970xd}, obtained from covariance under conformal maps.
	Scale symmetry alone would allow here an infinite number of possibilities \cite{Rychkov:2016iqz}.
	Measurements of infinite-volume 3pt functions are complicated, and it was never attempted.\footnote{We thank Thomas Chalopin and David Cl\'ement for a discussion of the possibility of realizing such a measurement in their experimental cold-atom platform \cite{Allemand:2025pdq}.} See Fig.~\ref{fig:flat}.
	
	Let us move to finite volume case, which is most relevant for the Rydberg atom experiments, typically done on groups of 20-200 atoms grouped in a 1D or 2D array. As we wish to compare with 2d CFT, we consider the 1D setup---a chain of Rydberg atoms arranged either as a circle or as an interval, whose interactions are adjusted to be at the quantum critical point. This corresponds to CFT on, respectively, the ``cylinder'' or the ``strip'' geometry (see Fig.~\ref{fig:cyl}), with time running vertically.  
	
		\begin{figure}[ht]
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=200pt]{fig-cyl.pdf}
		\caption{Direct tests of conformality in finite volume, discussed in the text. Left: 2pt function on the cylinder $S^1\times \mathbb{R}$. Right: 1pt function on the strip $[0,\pi]\times \mathbb{R}$ with identical boundary conditions.}%
		\label{fig:cyl}
	\end{figure}
	
	While scale symmetry is broken by finite volume, conformality survives. In fact conformal group for the 2d cylinder is as large as that of the plane, while for the strip it's the same as for the half-plane. This results in constraints on correlators.  As the simplest nontrivial example, consider the 2pt function of a primary on the cylinder \cite{Luscher:1974ez}:
	\beq
	\label{eq:2ptcyl}
	\langle \mathcal{O}(\phi_1,\tau_1) \mathcal{O}(\phi_2,\tau_2)\rangle_{\text{cyl}}  = 
	\frac{1}{|2 \cosh(\tau_1-\tau_2) - 2 \cos(\phi_1-\phi_2)|^{\Delta_\cO}}\,,
		\eeq
		where $\phi_i\in [0,2\pi]$, $\tau_i\in \mathbb{R}$. This is for the Euclidean cylinder. The 2pt function is time-translation invariant, and decays exponentially at large time separation. This expression can be easily found mapping the cylinder to the plane by an exponential map, and using the infinite-plane result \eqref{eq:two-point} \cite{DiFrancesco:1997nk}. But, for the sake of the argument, one can also derive it independently by staying on the cylinder and using its conformal group. 
		
		
		For the Hamiltonian dynamics in real time, like in Rydberg atom experiments, one needs to analytically continue \eqref{eq:2ptcyl} to Lorentzian signature. Then the 2pt function exhibits oscillating behavior in the time direction \cite{Luscher:1974ez}. Below we will focus on the equal time case, in which case the 2pt function reduces to
	\beq
	\label{eq:2ptcylequal}
	\langle \mathcal{O}(\phi_1,0) \mathcal{O}(\phi_2,0)\rangle_{\text{cyl}}  = \frac 1{|2\sin\frac{\phi_1-\phi_2}2|^{2\Delta_\cO}},
	\eeq
for both the Euclidean and Lorentzian signature.
	
	 In the same spirit, we can consider correlation functions on a strip, assuming conformal boundary conditions $B_1$ and $B_2$ on the two boundaries. In this case, already 1pt functions can test conformal symmetry. In the simplest case with equal boundary conditions, $B_1=B_2$, the 1pt function of a primary operator on the strip of width $\pi$ takes the form:
	 \beq
	 \label{eq:1ptstrip}
	 \langle \mathcal{O}(\phi,0)\rangle_{\text{strip}}  = \frac {A_{\mathcal{O}}}
	 {(2 \sin \phi)^{\Delta_\cO}}\,, \quad 0<\phi<\pi\,\quad(B_1=B_2)\,.
	 \eeq
	 This is usually shown conformal-transforming the upper-half-plane 1pt function $\langle \mathcal{O}(z)\rangle_{\text{UHP}} =  {A_{\mathcal{O}}}/(2\, \text{Im} z)^{\Delta_\cO}$ \cite{Cardy:1984bb} to the strip \cite{Calabrese:2006rx}. But it can also be derived by staying on the strip.
	  
	 For a nontrivial test, $A_\cO$ in \eqref{eq:1ptstrip} should not vanish due to global symmetry reasons. For example, for $\cO=\sigma$ in the 2d Ising CFT, the boundary condition should break global $\mathbb{Z}_2$, hence it should be either ``spin-up'' or ``spin-down'' boundary condition $|+\rangle$, $|-\rangle$ and not the ``free'' boundary condition $|f\rangle$.
	 
	 In summary, it is because of conformality that the functional form of \eqref{eq:2ptcyl}, \eqref{eq:1ptstrip} is fixed for all angles and not just in the short-distance limit where operators approach each other or the boundary, where it follows by scale symmetry. To see this functional form experimentally would be a direct test of conformality. In Section \ref{sec:prop} we will discuss further these two simplest direct tests. More complicated tests could be constructed using higher-point functions on the cylinder, or correlators with unequal boundary conditions on two sides of the strip or with boundary operators insertions. 
	
Let us next discuss another possible test of conformality, namely testing the operator-state correspondence (OSC). Consider all local operators of a CFT ${\cO}_i$, both primaries and their descendants. The OSC on the cylinder says that such operators are in one-to-one correspondence with the energy eigenstates on the circle, and that scaling dimensions and the gaps above the ground state are proportional to each other. There is also OSC for the strip with boundary conditions $B_1$ and $B_2$, between states on the interval and  boundary (or boundary-changing if $B_1\ne B_2$) operators $\widehat{\cO}_i$. 

The proportionality factor involves the ``speed of light'' of the emergent Lorentz invariance, as well as the physical length of space. To eliminate these factors, one can study the energy gap ratios. Then the prediction is
\beq
\label{eq:ratio-rel}
(E_i-E_0)/(E_j-E_0) = \Delta_{\cO_i}/\Delta_{\cO_j}\,\quad\text{(cylinder)},
\eeq	
and a similar relation with $\Delta_{\cO_i}\to\Delta_{\widehat{\cO}_i}$ for the strip.

OSC is a fundamental property of CFT, which results because the cylinder and strip geometries are conformally equivalent to the plane and half-plane (see Fig.~\ref{fig:OSC}). Therefore, a test of relation \eqref{eq:ratio-rel} also qualifies as a direct test of conformality.\footnote{We thank the authors of \cite{Wang:2026prw} for a discussion.}

	\begin{figure}[ht]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=200pt]{fig-OSC.pdf}
	\caption{Justification of OSC. Cylinder, resp. strip, can be conformally mapped onto the plane (left), resp. half-plane (right), with constant time sections mapped to constant radius (semi)circles.}%
	\label{fig:OSC}
\end{figure}

 We note that the discussed finite volume tests of conformal invariance have no easy counterpart for 2D, where finite volume typically breaks conformality. The only exception is a sphere, but in cold atom experiments spherical symmetry will be broken to a discrete subgroup, which makes comparison to CFT subtle although not impossible \cite{Lao:2023zis,Wu:2026ayb}. Experimental realization of the recently proposed fuzzy sphere quantum criticality \cite{Zhu:2022gjc}, with electrons moving on the sphere with a magnetic flux, is currently lacking.

\subsection{Existing experimental results: Rydberg atoms and related platforms}

Measurements of critical 2pt functions were performed by Refs.~\cite{Fang:2024uyf,Emperauger:2025raf}.
Ref.~\cite{Fang:2024uyf} studied the 2d Ising universality class with $N=24$ Rydberg atoms in a circle, while Ref.~\cite{Emperauger:2025raf} focused on the XY universality class in the same geometry.
Both works saw a short-distance powerlaw, followed by exponential decay with correlation length $\xi=13.2^{+5.7}_{-3.6}$ \cite{Fang:2024uyf} or $\xi=15(4)$ \cite{Emperauger:2025raf}, attributed to decoherence effects and other imperfections. Thus the direct test of conformality via Eq.~\eqref{eq:2ptcylequal} remains open. (See Section \ref{sec:prop} for possible improvements.) Still, from short-distance powerlaw they could extract scaling dimensions and compare with the theory. Ref.~\cite{Fang:2024uyf} saw $\Delta_\sigma=0.127(37)$ in agreement with the 2d Ising CFT, which counts as an indirect test of conformality. Ref.~\cite{Emperauger:2025raf} measured $\Delta\approx 0.13$, the vertex operator dimension in the compact scalar boson CFT at Luttinger parameter $K=1.6(4)$. Since $K$ is a modulus (unconstrained parameter), it's hard to interpret this result as an indirect test of conformality.

Energy gap measurements were performed in Ref.~\cite{Sun:2026aqf}, working on the interval geometry (a chain of $N=19$ Rydberg atoms), and using time-periodic modulation. They thus tested the OSC relation \eqref{eq:ratio-rel} for the strip. For the critical Ising model with spin-up boundary conditions on both sides, they measured low energy gaps up to level $\sim7$, showing that they are are equally spaced, in agreement with the free Majorana theory description of the underlying CFT. They next studied the tricritical Ising (TCI) model with several boundary conditions. E.g.~for TCI with the free boundary conditions they measured $(E_2-E_0)/(E_1-E_0)=1.45(25)$ in agreement with the CFT prediction $4/3$. As the TCI boundary spectrum does not have a free theory description, {\bf this is a nontrivial direct test of conformality}, perhaps the first one ever.

The measurements of Ref.~\cite{Fang:2024uyf,Emperauger:2025raf,Sun:2026aqf} were done with the critical ground state, prepared via with adiabatic evolution. Another setup is to drive a 1D \cite{Keesling:2018ish,Zhang:2025xkp} or 2D \cite{Ebadi:2020ldi,Manovitz:2024hif} array of Rydberg atoms through the critical point non-adiabatically, and extract critical exponents from the Kibble-Zurek scaling \cite{delCampo:2013nla}. As any exponent measurement, these count as indirect tests of conformality, including its prediction $z=1$ for the dynamical critical exponent. 

The Kibble-Zurek scaling for the 1D transverse field Ising model driven through the quantum critical point was also studied using a D-Wave quantum annealer \cite{King:2022phl} but we are not aware of correlator measurements using this experimental platform.

We also mention closely related digital quantum computer studies \cite{Anand:2022cdi,Dborin:2022zdd,Haghshenas:2023bje}. They first use a classical computer to find a holographic quantum circuit describing the critical state of interest, e.g.~the 2d Ising criticality. The circuit is then evaluated on a quantum computer, measuring e.g.~the 2pt function. While these works are not fully experimental tests of conformality in a quantum phase transition, they do demonstrate growing power of quantum computing.

 

\section{Simple setups for direct tests of conformality}
\label{sec:prop}

We consider the standard setup known to lead to Ising quantum criticality in 1d chains of Rydberg atoms \cite[Eq.~(1)]{Browaeys:2020kzz}. We will be interested in periodic circular ring or linear chain arrangements, with equal spacing $a$ between neighboring atoms.

The ground and Rydberg states of each atom $|g_i\rangle$ and $|r_i\rangle$ are identified with down and up spin-$1/2$ states. 
The Hamiltonian for the chain of atoms is
\begin{equation}
	\hat H_{phys}=\frac{\hbar \Omega}{2} \sum_i X_i - \hbar \Delta \sum_i n_i +  \sum_{i<j}V_{ij} n_{i}n_{j},\quad V_{ij}= \frac{C_6}{R_{ij}^6},
	\label{eq:model}
\end{equation}
where $\Omega$ is the effective Rabi frequency, $\Delta$ is the detuning, $n_i=|r_i\rangle \langle r_i|$ counts Rydberg excitations at site $i$,  $X_i$ the Pauli-x matrix, $R_{ij}=|\mathbf{r}_i-\mathbf{r}_j|$ is the distance between the atoms, and $V_{ij}$ is the van der Waals interaction assumed repulsive, $C_6>0$.


Denoting $U=C_6/a^6$, we will work with the dimensionless Hamiltonian $\hat H=\hat H_{phys}/U$:
\begin{align}
	\hat H &= \frac{\Omega'}{2} \sum_i X_i  - \Delta' \sum_i n_i + \sum_{i<j}\frac{1}{d_{ij}^6}  n_{i}n_{j}
	\label{eq:modelp}
\end{align}
where $\Omega'=\hbar \Omega/U$ and $\Delta'=\hbar \Delta/U$ are real dimensionless numbers, and $d_{ij}=R_{ij}/a$ is the dimensionless distance. 

The rich phase diagram of this model is well studied \cite{Keesling:2018ish,Rader:2019syq}. Most of it is occupied by the disordered phase, the $\mathbb{Z}_2$-broken density wave phase, and the line of Ising critical points separating them (Fig.~\ref{phasediagramHBlong}). This part of the phase diagram can be understood rewriting the Hamiltonian in terms of the sigma-matrices via $n_i=(1+Z_i)/2$:
\beq
\hat H = \frac{\Omega'}{2} \sum_i X_i + \sum_{i<j}\frac{1}{4d_{ij}^6}  Z_{i}Z_{j}+  \sum_{i}  \left(\sum_j \frac{1}{4d_{ij}^6} - \frac{\Delta'}{2}\right) Z_{i} + const
\eeq
If $\Omega',\Delta'$ are not too small, we can neglect non-nearest-neighbor $\sigma^z_{i}\sigma^z_{j}$ couplings. This gives the antiferromagnetic Ising model in longitudinal and transverse magnetic fields, which has a very similar phase diagram \cite{Ovchinnikov}. In particular, in the infinite-volume limit, the longitudinal field vanishes for $\Delta'=\zeta(6)\approx 1.017$ and the phase diagram is symmetric with respect to this line. ($\Omega'\to -\Omega'$ is also a symmetry.)

The strong Rydberg blockade limit $\Omega',\Delta'\ll 1$ requires a separate treatment. This can be studied via the effective Fendley-Sengupta-Sachdev (FSS) model \cite{FSS} or directly from \eqref{eq:modelp} \cite{Keesling:2018ish,Rader:2019syq}. One finds, for $\Delta'\lesssim 0.05$, $\Omega'\lesssim 0.01$, a hierarchy of crystalline phases of period 3,4,5,\ldots, as well as non-commensurate ``floating'' phases separating them. 

Here we will focus on the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ critical line. Everywhere on this line, the phase transition is in the Ising universality class, although the Hamiltonian $\hat H$ does not generically have onsite $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry. At a generic point, the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry of the Ising CFT emerges (or ``emanates'' \cite{Seiberg:2023cdc}) from the one-unit lattice translation \cite{Slagle:2021ene},  which permutes the two ground states in the broken phase. The only exception is $\Delta'=\zeta(6)$ where the Hamiltonian does have onsite $\mathbb{Z}_2$: $Z_i\to -Z_i$. 

Several experimental works, including the recent Refs.~\cite{Fang:2024uyf,Sun:2026aqf}, studied the Ising transition at generic points of the critical line, towards the strong Rydberg blockade region (see Fig.~\ref{phasediagramHBlong}). Here we will instead focus on the point $\Delta'=\zeta(6)$, $\Omega'\approx 0.488$, far from the Rydberg blockade. In addition to onsite $\mathbb{Z}_2$, this point has other advantages discussed below.

\subsection{Two-point function on a circle: DMRG prediction}

We now consider the arrangement of atoms along a circle which was studied in \cite{Fang:2024uyf,Emperauger:2025raf}.
Since we are on a circle the dimensionless distance in \eqref{eq:model} is $d_{ij}=\frac{\sin(\pi |i-j|/N)}{\sin(\pi/N)}$.

We are interested in the CFT 2pt function at equal time $\langle \sigma(\phi_1,0)\sigma(\phi_2,0)\rangle$, see Eq.~\eqref{eq:2ptcylequal}, where $\sigma$ is the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ nontrivial primary of the 2d Ising CFT. Correlation functions of $\epsilon$ can also be discussed, but since $\epsilon$ has a higher scaling dimension, they will decay faster with a distance and the powerlaw will be harder to resolve experimentally (more on this below).

As mentioned we work at $\Delta'=\zeta(6)$ where the lattice model has onsite $\mathbb{Z}_2$ which flips the sign of $Z_i$. The operators even and odd under this on-site symmetry will map at long distances to $\mathbb{Z}_2$ even and odd CFT operators. So e.g.
\beq
Z_j = b (-1)^j \sigma(\phi) +\ldots,\quad \phi=2\pi j/N,
\eeq 
where $b$ is a non-universal constant and $(-1)^j$ factor transforms the antiferromagnetic spin chain to the ferromagnetic one. Importantly, the terms $\ldots$ may not contain $\mathbb{Z}_2$ even operators. Taking into account spatial parity, the lowest-dimension operator in $\ldots$ is $(-1)^j \partial_\phi^2 \sigma(\phi)$.

So, we use $(-1)^j Z_j$ as a microscopic proxy for $\sigma(\phi)$. To study $\langle\sigma(0) \sigma(\phi)\rangle$ we measure $(-1)^j\langle Z_0 Z_{j}\rangle$ in the ground state of the spin chain, which we obtain via DMRG. This is shown in Fig.~\ref{ZZ2pt}, plotted with respect to $d_{0j}$ which maps to $\sin(\phi/2)$ up to a constant factor. We take $N=24$ as in \cite{Fang:2024uyf,Emperauger:2025raf}.

\subsection{Estimation of needed sample size}

The DMRG calculation provides access to the ground state wavefunction
\beq
\Psi_{\rm GS} = \sum_{n_i=0,1} a_{n_0,\ldots, n_{L-1}} |n_0,\ldots, n_{L-1}\rangle
\eeq
The correlation function observables are computed averaging with respect to this wavefunction.

In the experiment we will not have access to the wavefunction itself but to \emph{collapsed states} $\Psi_a$, $a=1,\ldots,M$. Each $\Psi_a$ is one of the basis states in the $n_i=0,1$ basis and they will appear in the experiment according to the Born rule, i.e. probability to observe a collapsed state $|n_0,\ldots, n_{L-1}\rangle$ equals $|a_{n_0,\ldots, n_{L-1}}|^2$. 

Any observable which is a function of $n_i$'s can be obtained experimentally averaging over a sufficiently large number of collapsed states. The 2pt function in Fig.~\ref{ZZ2pt} is such an observable. How many collapsed states is needed for a good signal-to-noise ratio? I.e.~how many times $M$ shall we have to repeat the experiment? To answer this question, we need to study the \emph{variance} of our observable. 

The variance of any observable $\mathcal{O}$ can be computed from the expected value of $\mathcal{O}^2$. Generally, at large distances a correlator will tend to zero, because we arrange the 1pt function of lattice operators corresponding to CFT operators to be zero. However, the 1pt function of the \emph{squares} of the same operators will not be zero, causing the correlator variance to go to a constant in the same limit. So, good statistics will be hardest to obtain in the large-distance tails, as expected.

	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{ZZ_correlation_est.png}
	\caption{Estimating the critical 2pt function on the ring of $N=24$ atoms from independent snapshots. 
The red dots DMRG data and the dashed line CFT prediction are the same as in Fig.~\ref{ZZ2pt}, while the gray error bars are the confidence intervals \eqref{eq:conf} for one random sample of size $M=2\times 10^3$. 
		\SR{adjust axes lables to $(-1)^j \langle  Z_0 Z_{j}\rangle$ and $d_{0j}$. Add DMRG points in red. Make error bars gray and remove the central dot from error bars.}  }
	\label{ZZ2ptsample}
\end{figure}

Here we will not compute the variance but will estimate it, using a method which is more direct and closer to the experiment. DMRG algorithm produces the wavefunction as an MPS which one can quickly \emph{sample}, i.e. to generate sequences of random collapsed states (``snapshots'') distributed according to the Born rule.\footnote{The {\tt sample()} function of {\tt ITensor} library \cite{ITensor} does that. A similar algorithm was used in \cite[App.B]{Scholl:2020hzx}. } This is very quick and allows us to simulate the experimental process directly. Statistical errors will go down as $\Sigma/\sqrt{M}$, where $M$ is the number of snapshots, with $\Sigma$ estimated from the "DMRG data".

For each of $M$ collected collapsed states $\Psi_a$ we measure the 2pt function $(-1)^j\langle Z_0 Z_{j}\rangle$ as a function of $j$ (averaging over translations):
\beq\label{sample}
X_{a}(j)=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=0}^{N-1} \langle \Psi_a|(-1)^j Z_i Z_{i+j}|\Psi_a\rangle.
\eeq
$X_{a}(j)$ for each $a$ are independent identically distributed random variables, whose mean $\overline{X(j)}$ is the ground-state 2pt function $\langle \Psi|(-1)^j Z_0 Z_{j}|\Psi\rangle$. The mean and the standard deviation $\Sigma$ are estimated by the standard formulas
\beq
	\overline{X(j)}_{est}=\frac{1}{M} \sum_{a} X_{a}(j),\qquad
\Sigma(j)^2_{est} = \frac{1}{M-1}\sum_i (X_a(j) - \overline{X(j)})^2\,.
\eeq
By the central limit theorem, for $M\to\infty$, the sample mean $\overline{X(j)}_{est}$ has a gaussian distribution with variance $ (\Sigma(j)/\sqrt{M})^2$ around the true mean.  We therefore obtain an estimation of the 2pt correlator, including one standard deviation error bars, as
\beq
(-1)^j \langle  Z_0 Z_{j}\rangle_{est} = \overline{X(x)}_{est}\pm \frac{1}{\sqrt{M}} \sqrt{\Sigma(x)^2_{est}}.
\label{eq:conf}
\eeq
In Fig.~\ref{ZZ2ptsample} we show the result of applying this procedure to the critical 2pt function for $N=24$ and $M=2\times 10^3$ samples. 
From the agreement with Fig.~\ref{ZZ2pt}, we conclude that $O(10^3)$ repetitions should be sufficient to resolve the shape of this correlator.
